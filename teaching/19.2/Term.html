This is to be an expository paper, on a topic involving mathematics or mathematics education, written for a reader with a similar mathematical background as your fellow students. I have a list at the end of this page of some suggestions; feel free to come up with your own, and also feel free to talk to me about that. The paper need not contain proofs, but should have significant mathematical content, but for some topics, you will need to give some proofs, or a sketch, and for some you will need graphics to illustrate the main ideas. Any mathematics should be explained clearly enough that your classmates would understand it. You must use and cite two or more sources (e.g. books, journals, or newspaper articles), not including Wikipedia, but you should look at on-line sources as well. Numberphile may be a source for inspiration.
If your paper includes any mathematics, you should typeset it in LaTeX, which you should start learning how to use. All assignments are to be typeset in some manner (please nothing handwritten), and handed in on paper with your name on each page and page numbers if it is more than one page long.
Due dates and further details are below.
Friday 6 September Proposal due at or before the start of class.
This should be one or two sentences describing the chosen topic.
Monday 30 September     Outline of paper due at or before the start of class. It should contain most or all of the ideas that will appear in the final paper.
Include references. Any reasonable style is OK.
Monday 21 October Rough draft of paper due at or before the start of class.
Friday 1 November Peer Review.
Monday 18 November Final paper due at or before the start of class. This must be a minimum of 2000 words, typed (approximately 8 pages if double spaced).
The grade will be based on mechanics, presentation, and content.

Here are some topics to get you thinking

  • The Five Color Theorem. (this should include history and a proof).
  • The Four Color Theorem.
  • Crystal structures and symmetry.
  • Any Fields medalist (including the most recent ones).
  • Any mathematician for which one can write 5–10 pages.
  • Any of the Millennium Prize problems.
  • Euler Characteristic.
  • Gödel Incompleteness Theorem.
  • Cantor's theory of infinite sets.
  • Continuum hypothesis.
  • Error-correcting codes, especially linear or matrix codes.
  • public key cryptography.
  • Prime number theorem.
  • Sphere packing problem (Kepler's conjecture).
  • Platonic solids in all dimensions.
  • Transcendental Numbers.
  • Big theorems or problems such as Fermat's last theorem, The Poincare conjecture, or the Riemann Hypothesis.
  • Other important applications of Mathematics.
  • P vs. NP.
  • Democratic elections and Arrow theorem.
  • Proofs of impossibility (e.g. classical Greek constructions).
  • The numbers 17, 257, and 65537.
